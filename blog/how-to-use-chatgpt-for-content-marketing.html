How to Use ChatGPT for Content Marketing

Most marketers use ChatGPT like a search engine — one question, one answer, done. That's why their content sounds generic. Here's the 6-step workflow that actually produces publishable output.

The BuzzRiding Team Researched and refined with AI tools

Why most marketers get bad output from ChatGPT

The problem isn't ChatGPT. The problem is how most people use it. They open a blank chat, type "write me a blog post about [topic]", get a generic 800-word article with a list of obvious points, and conclude AI content doesn't work.

The output you get is a direct reflection of the input you give. Vague prompt — vague article. Specific, structured prompt — specific, structured article. The workflow below is the difference between the two.

⏱ Time Investment

Total active time for this workflow: 25–35 minutes per article. The rest is waiting for AI output and light reading. You can go from keyword to published-ready draft in under 90 minutes total, including your own review.

The 6-step ChatGPT content marketing workflow

Competitor gap analysis

Before you write a word, understand what's already ranking and where the gaps are. Feed ChatGPT the top 5 competing articles on your target keyword and ask it to find what they all miss.

Prompt to use
Here are summaries of the top 5 articles ranking for "[your keyword]": [paste summaries]. Identify: (1) what topics they all cover, (2) what they all miss or gloss over, (3) what questions they don't answer. List 10 reader FAQs based on gaps you find.

Build the content brief

A brief is a 5-bullet document that tells ChatGPT exactly what to write. Don't skip this step — jumping straight to a draft without a brief is why output is generic.

Prompt to use
Based on the gaps above, write a content brief for an article targeting "[keyword]". Include: target reader (mid-level marketing professional), unique angle that beats competitors, 5-6 H2 section topics, and 3 specific data points or examples to include.

Write the full draft

Now write the article — but give ChatGPT the brief, not just the keyword. This is the single most important change most marketers can make to their AI workflow.

Prompt to use
Using this brief: [paste brief]. Write a 1,500-word article targeting "[keyword]". Voice: friendly, data-informed, practical, jargon-free. Structure: 2-sentence hook intro stating the problem, 5-6 H2 sections, FAQ section with 5 questions. Do not use filler phrases like "in today's world" or "it's worth noting".

The 15-minute human pass

This is non-negotiable. Read the draft and do three things: rewrite the intro in your own words, add one real specific insight or example from your own experience, and remove any sentence that sounds like it was written by a committee.

You're not rewriting the whole article — you're making it sound like a person wrote it. 15 minutes is enough.

Generate all SEO metadata

Once the article is finalised, get ChatGPT to write everything needed for publishing in one prompt.

Prompt to use
For this article: [paste article title and intro]. Write: (1) meta title under 60 characters including the keyword, (2) meta description under 155 characters, (3) 3 URL slug options, (4) 5 internal link anchor text suggestions for related articles.

Turn one article into a week of content

One article should generate at least 7 pieces of social content. This step takes 5 minutes and fills your Buffer queue for the week.

Prompt to use
Here is my article: [paste article]. Repurpose it into: (1) 3 LinkedIn posts — each with a hook line, 4 bullet takeaways, and a CTA to read more. (2) 1 X/Twitter thread of 6 tweets. (3) 2 Instagram captions under 150 words each. (4) 1 newsletter teaser of 150 words with a pull quote.

"The workflow is the product. A good prompt once is luck. A repeatable system is a competitive advantage."

Common mistakes that kill your output quality

After running this workflow on dozens of articles, these are the mistakes that consistently produce bad output:

  • Skipping the brief. Jumping from keyword to draft is the single biggest output quality killer. The brief is what separates a generic article from one that has a clear angle.
  • Accepting the first draft. AI first drafts are starting points. The human edit in Step 4 is what makes content worth reading.
  • Generic voice instructions. "Write in a friendly tone" is useless. Give ChatGPT examples of sentences you want and sentences you don't want. Specificity produces specificity.
  • Skipping the repurpose step. Most marketers write the article and stop. The repurpose step takes 5 minutes and extends the life of every piece of content across every channel.

How to make the output sound less AI-generated

The tell-tale signs of AI content are predictable: overly balanced arguments, no strong opinions, generic examples, and intros that start with "In today's fast-paced world." Here's how to fix each one.

Add one specific number. "Many marketers use AI" becomes "67% of marketing teams now use at least one AI writing tool" — immediately more credible.

Take a position. Instead of "there are pros and cons to both tools," say "Tool A is better for X, and here's exactly why." Readers came for a recommendation, not a balanced summary.

Rewrite the intro yourself. The intro is the one part where your voice matters most. 3 sentences, in your own words, stating the problem clearly. Everything after that can be AI-assisted.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use ChatGPT to write entire blog posts?
Yes — but with a human edit pass. AI-only content without review tends to be generic, overly balanced, and free of the specific insights that make articles worth reading. The 6-step workflow above produces publishable content because it combines AI efficiency with a focused human review at the right point.
Will Google penalise AI-generated content?
Google's guidance is that it rewards helpful, high-quality content regardless of how it was produced. AI content that is thin, generic, or created purely to rank without helping readers is what gets penalised — not AI content that is genuinely useful. The human edit in Step 4 is what keeps your content on the right side of this line.
Is ChatGPT or Claude better for content marketing?
Both work for this workflow. Claude tends to produce better long-form output with more nuanced brand voice matching. ChatGPT is stronger for short-form and structured tasks like subject line variants. For the 6-step workflow above, either tool works — the workflow matters more than the model.
How long does the full 6-step workflow take?
25–35 minutes of active time, 60–90 minutes total including AI generation time and reading. Steps 1–3 take about 15 minutes. The human edit (Step 4) takes 15 minutes. SEO and repurposing (Steps 5–6) take around 10 minutes combined.
What's the best ChatGPT prompt for content marketing?
There isn't one best prompt — there's a best sequence of prompts. A single "write me an article" prompt will always produce generic output. The workflow above breaks content creation into 6 focused prompts, each building on the last. That's what produces publishable output.
