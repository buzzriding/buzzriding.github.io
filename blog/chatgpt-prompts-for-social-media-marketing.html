ChatGPT Prompts for Social Media Marketing: 25 That Actually Work

BuzzRiding Team
8 min read
March 2026

Most ChatGPT prompt guides give you vague instructions like "write a LinkedIn post about marketing." That's not a prompt — that's a search query. This guide gives you 25 specific, tested prompts across LinkedIn, X, Instagram, and Pinterest — ready to use with zero fluff.

Why Most Social Media Prompts Fail

The problem isn't ChatGPT. The problem is how most marketers prompt it. Vague inputs produce vague outputs — generic captions that sound exactly like AI wrote them, which readers can spot immediately.

The fix is specificity. Every good prompt has four elements: a clear role for the AI, a defined audience, a specific format, and a constraint that forces quality. The prompts below follow this structure.

One caveat: these are starting points. Plan for one round of editing — 5 minutes per post — to add your voice and a real example.

LinkedIn Prompts (8 Prompts)

LinkedIn rewards insight, not polish. The best-performing posts lead with a counterintuitive observation, follow with a short story or data point, and end with a question that invites comments.

Hook-First Post

Prompt

"Write a LinkedIn post for a B2B marketing manager audience. Start with a one-sentence hook that challenges a common assumption about [topic]. Follow with 4 bullet points of practical insight. End with a question that invites comments. Max 200 words. No hashtags. No emojis."

Data-Led Post

Prompt

"Write a LinkedIn post sharing this finding: [paste your stat or research]. Explain why it matters to a mid-level marketing professional. Use a short intro, 3 bullet points with implications, and a CTA to follow for more like this. Conversational tone, no jargon."

Story Post

Prompt

"Write a LinkedIn story post about [experience or lesson]. Structure: one-sentence opener with a surprising detail, 3-paragraph story arc (problem, action, result), one takeaway the reader can apply today. Under 250 words. First-person voice, professional but warm."

Contrarian Take

Prompt

"Write a LinkedIn post that argues against the common advice that [widely accepted marketing belief]. Present a specific, well-reasoned counter-argument. Back it up with one example or data point. Keep it under 180 words. Don't hedge — commit to the point of view."

Tool Recommendation

Prompt

"Write a LinkedIn post recommending [tool name] to marketing professionals who haven't tried it yet. Start with the specific problem it solves. Give one real use case. Name one limitation honestly. End with who should and shouldn't use it. Under 200 words."

List Post

Prompt

"Write a LinkedIn list post: '5 things I wish I knew about [topic] before I started.' Each point should be specific and actionable — no generic advice. Lead each bullet with a bold phrase. End with a question. Under 220 words."

Repurposed Article

Prompt

"I've written an article about [topic]. Here's the key section: [paste]. Turn this into a standalone LinkedIn post that works without reading the article. Lead with the most surprising insight. Add a CTA: 'Full breakdown in the comments.' Under 180 words."

Weekly Roundup

Prompt

"Write a LinkedIn weekly roundup post covering: [3 bullet points of news or insights]. Format: short intro, 3 items with a one-line take on each, one closing observation. Professional but not boring. Under 230 words."

X / Twitter Prompts (6 Prompts)

X rewards brevity and a distinct point of view. The best-performing posts make one point clearly, with no filler. Threads work when each tweet earns the next click — not just continues a paragraph.

Single Tweet

Prompt

"Write a single tweet (under 280 characters) that shares this insight: [your insight]. Make it punchy. No hashtags. No 'Thread 🧵' opener. End with something that makes people want to follow."

Thread Opener

Prompt

"Write the opening tweet of a thread about [topic]. It should make a bold claim or tease a surprising finding. Under 200 characters. Must make the reader want to click 'show more.' No emojis unless absolutely necessary."

Full Thread

Prompt

"Write a 6-tweet thread about [topic] for a marketing professional audience. Tweet 1: bold claim or surprising stat. Tweets 2–5: one specific, actionable point each. Tweet 6: summary and CTA to follow. Each tweet under 260 characters. No hashtag spam."

Quote Tweet Response

Prompt

"Write a quote tweet response to this post: [paste post]. Add a contrasting perspective or an interesting nuance the original missed. Keep it under 220 characters. Respectful but direct."

Product / Tool Post

Prompt

"Write a tweet recommending [tool] to marketers. One clear benefit, one real use case, one link. Under 240 characters. Sounds like a human recommendation, not an ad."

Hot Take

Prompt

"Write a tweet with a hot take on [topic in marketing]. Should be mildly controversial — something that splits opinion but has a solid argument behind it. Under 200 characters. No hedging."

Instagram Prompts (6 Prompts)

Instagram captions have more room than most marketers use. The best ones open with a strong line visible before the 'more' cut, then deliver real value, then close with a question.

Educational Caption

Prompt

"Write an Instagram caption explaining [concept] to a marketing professional audience. Opening line (before 'more' cut): make it a question or surprising stat. Body: 4–5 short sentences with one actionable tip. Close: a simple question to boost comments. 150 words max. 3–5 relevant hashtags at the end."

Behind the Process

Prompt

"Write an Instagram caption in a 'behind the scenes' style about [process or workflow]. Conversational, first-person, honest. Show the messy reality, not just the polished result. Under 140 words. End with a question asking followers about their experience."

Carousel Introduction

Prompt

"Write an Instagram caption to introduce a carousel post about [topic]. The caption should tease the value inside the carousel without giving it away. Under 100 words. End with 'Swipe to see all [number].' 4 relevant hashtags."

Tool Review

Prompt

"Write an Instagram caption reviewing [tool name]. Format: 1 sentence on what it does, 2 sentences on what's good, 1 sentence on the limitation, 1 sentence on who should use it. Under 130 words. Honest tone, not promotional."

Quote Graphic

Prompt

"Write a short Instagram caption (under 80 words) to accompany a quote graphic. The quote is: [your quote]. Briefly explain why this matters to marketers right now. End with a thought-provoking question. 3 hashtags max."

Repurposed Article

Prompt

"I've written a blog post about [topic]. Here's the intro: [paste]. Rewrite this as an Instagram caption. Keep the best insight, cut all filler. Under 120 words. Add a CTA: 'Full article — link in bio.' 4 hashtags."

Pinterest Prompts (5 Prompts)

Pinterest is a search engine, not a social network. Descriptions should be keyword-rich and written as if the reader is searching for exactly this content — because they are.

Article Pin Description

Prompt

"Write a Pinterest pin description for an article titled '[article title].' Target keyword: [keyword]. Format: 2–3 sentences describing what the reader will learn. Include the keyword naturally. Under 100 words. No clickbait."

How-To Pin

Prompt

"Write a Pinterest description for a how-to guide about [topic]. Target keyword: 'how to [action].' Start with the outcome the reader achieves. List 2–3 things they'll learn. Under 100 words. Clear and specific."

Tool Comparison Pin

Prompt

"Write a Pinterest description comparing [tool A] vs [tool B] for marketers. Target keyword: '[tool A] vs [tool B].' 2 sentences on the comparison, 1 sentence on who each suits. Under 90 words. SEO-optimised."

Infographic Pin

Prompt

"Write a Pinterest description for an infographic about [topic]. Target keywords: [keyword 1], [keyword 2]. Describe the visual content and its value to a marketing professional. 80–100 words. Written as if someone is searching for this content right now."

Evergreen Tip Pin

Prompt

"Write a Pinterest description for a tip about [topic]. Target keyword: [keyword]. One sentence on the problem, one sentence on the solution, one sentence on the result. Under 80 words. Timeless — no date references."

How to Get Better Results Every Time

Even with strong prompts, you'll occasionally get output that doesn't land. Here's how to diagnose it:

  • Too generic? Add more context. Paste in your actual data, a real tool name, or a specific audience detail.
  • Sounds like AI? Ask ChatGPT to "rewrite this in a more conversational, first-person tone — like a senior marketer explaining it to a colleague."
  • Too long? Add a hard word count to your prompt. It takes them seriously.
  • Wrong tone? Add a "tone reference" — paste in a post you like and say "match this tone."

One rule that makes everything better: Always add "do not use filler phrases like 'In today's fast-paced world' or 'It's worth noting'" to every prompt. It cuts AI-speak almost entirely.

Building a Prompt Library

The most efficient approach is to build your own prompt library in a Google Doc or Notion page — one section per platform. As you find variations that work for your brand, save them. Within a month, you'll have a personalised prompt system that consistently produces on-brand output.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are these prompts specific to ChatGPT or do they work with other AI tools?
They work with any major AI writing tool — Claude, Gemini, Jasper, and others. The principles (specificity, role definition, format constraints) are universal. Some tools respond differently to tone instructions, so test and adjust.
How much editing should I expect to do after using these prompts?
Plan for 3–5 minutes of editing per post. The most common edits: replacing generic examples with real ones, adjusting tone to match your brand voice, and tightening the opening line.
Can I use these prompts to write posts in bulk?
Yes — and this is where the time savings stack up. Run one article through the LinkedIn, X, and Instagram prompts in a single session and you'll have 8–10 posts in under 30 minutes. Paste them into Buffer and you're done for the week.
Will AI-generated posts get penalised by LinkedIn or Instagram algorithms?
There's no evidence platforms algorithmically penalise AI-written content. What they do penalise is low-quality, generic content — which is why editing and personalisation matter. The issue is quality, not origin.
What's the best platform to start with if I'm new to AI social media prompts?
Start with LinkedIn. The format is forgiving, the audience responds to insight-led content, and the prompts above produce strong first drafts. Once you're comfortable editing AI output for LinkedIn, the other platforms follow naturally.
