I Used AI to Write a Month of Blog Posts — Here's What Happened

BuzzRiding Team
9 min read
March 2026

For 30 days, I handed my entire blog content workflow over to AI. No ghostwriters, no content agencies — just Claude and ChatGPT doing the heavy lifting. Here's the unfiltered breakdown of what happened: the output quality, the time saved, the SEO results, and the moments where AI completely fell flat.

The Setup

Before the experiment, my typical blog workflow looked like this: 2–3 hours per post, covering research, outline, drafting, editing, and SEO optimisation. I was publishing roughly two posts a week — which felt sustainable but left no bandwidth for anything else.

The goal of the experiment was simple: could I maintain the same publishing cadence, at comparable quality, while cutting my active working time by at least 50%? I set some ground rules:

  • Every post had to start as an AI-generated draft — no writing from scratch
  • I could edit, but not rewrite entire sections from scratch (that would defeat the point)
  • I'd add one real example or data point to each post that AI couldn't know
  • Every post had to pass my personal quality bar before publishing

Tools used: Claude for research briefs and drafts, ChatGPT for repurposing and social posts, Ahrefs Free for keyword targeting, Google Search Console for results tracking.

What Worked — Better Than Expected

Research briefs were the biggest win

Before writing anything, I asked Claude to analyse the top-ranking articles for my target keyword, identify the gaps they had, and list the questions readers were actually asking. What used to take 45 minutes of manual SERP analysis took about 4 minutes.

The quality was genuinely good. Claude identified angles that the competing articles missed — not always, but often enough to give my posts a differentiated entry point. This is where AI saves the most time with the least quality trade-off.

First drafts were 70% usable on first pass

I expected to do significant rewriting. Most of the time, I didn't need to. The structure was logical, the headings were sensible, and the body copy was competent. My editing typically involved:

  • Tightening the opening — AI tends to warm up slowly, I wanted a harder hook
  • Replacing generic examples with specific, real ones
  • Removing hedging language ("it's worth noting", "in many cases")
  • Adding one original observation the AI couldn't produce

On average, I spent 20–25 minutes editing per post. That's down from 60–90 minutes of drafting and editing combined.

Social repurposing became trivial

Previously, repurposing a blog post into social content took another 30–40 minutes. With AI, I'd paste the article and ask for a LinkedIn post, a Twitter thread, and two Instagram captions. Total time: under 10 minutes, including light editing. Over 8 posts, that's roughly 3–4 hours saved in repurposing alone.

What Didn't Work — The Honest Failures

AI can't replace first-hand experience

The posts that read most authentically were the ones where I added a real observation — something I'd actually tested, noticed, or experienced. When I skipped this step (I did, twice), the posts felt like competent summaries rather than original content. Readers can feel the difference, even if they can't articulate why.

Issue

Posts without a personal insight had noticeably lower engagement on social and lower average time-on-page in GA4.

Accuracy needs checking — every time

AI confidently stated two statistics in early drafts that I couldn't verify when I went to source them. Neither were wildly wrong, but neither were provably right either. I started adding a verification step to my workflow: any stat or claim I didn't already know to be true, I checked before publishing. This added about 8 minutes per post.

Issue

Never publish AI-generated statistics without verifying the source. If you can't find the source, cut the stat.

Tool comparison posts required more intervention

For posts comparing specific tools, AI's training data has a cutoff. Pricing, features, and product updates change constantly — and AI doesn't always know what's current. I had to manually verify every tool claim in comparison posts. These took longer than other post types.

Mixed

AI is good for structure and framing in comparison posts, but the actual data needs to come from you.

The "voice drift" problem

Over the month, I noticed that posts where I edited less tended to sound slightly different from posts where I edited more. The AI voice isn't bad — it's just not mine. Readers who follow a brand closely notice this. By week 3, I started giving Claude more explicit voice instructions: pasting in a section I'd written previously and saying "match this tone." This helped significantly.

The SEO Results (4 Weeks In)

Four weeks is too short to draw firm SEO conclusions — Google's indexing and ranking timeline runs longer than a month. But here's what the data showed at the 30-day mark:

  • 6 of 8 posts indexed within 72 hours of publishing
  • 2 posts appearing in Search Console for their target keyword within 4 weeks
  • Average position for early-ranking posts: 18–35 (expected range for new content)
  • No ranking at position 1–10 yet — but no red flags either

The honest SEO verdict: AI-assisted content doesn't appear to be penalised. The posts that ranked fastest were the ones with the clearest structure, specific headings, and FAQ sections — all things AI is genuinely good at producing.

The Workflow I'd Recommend

After 30 days of testing, here's the version of the workflow I'd actually use going forward — refined from what worked and what didn't:

  1. Research brief (AI): Ask Claude to analyse top-ranking articles, identify gaps, and list FAQs. 5 minutes.
  2. Keyword confirmation (you): Verify the target keyword in Ahrefs Free before writing. 3 minutes.
  3. Draft (AI): Generate the full article with specific voice and format instructions. 3–5 minutes.
  4. Add your insight (you): Insert one real observation, test result, or example only you could write. 10 minutes.
  5. Verify facts (you): Check any statistics or specific claims you didn't already know. 8 minutes.
  6. Edit for voice (you): Tighten the opening, remove hedging, adjust tone where needed. 15 minutes.
  7. SEO metadata (AI): Generate meta title, description, and internal link suggestions. 3 minutes.
  8. Repurpose (AI): Social posts and newsletter blurb. 8 minutes.

Total active time: approximately 55 minutes per post, including repurposing. That's down from 2.5–3 hours without AI. The quality, in my assessment, is comparable — and in some cases better, because the research phase is more thorough.

Would I Keep Doing This?

Yes — with the caveat that "AI-assisted" and "AI-replaced" are different things. The posts that performed best were the ones where AI handled the heavy lifting and I handled the editorial judgement. The moment I tried to reduce my involvement below that threshold, quality dropped noticeably.

The right mental model: AI is the research assistant and first-draft writer. You're still the editor, fact-checker, and the one injecting genuine insight. That split — roughly 80% AI effort, 20% human — produces surprisingly good output.

Frequently Asked Questions

Which AI tool — Claude or ChatGPT — produced better blog drafts?
Claude produced more structured, better-organised drafts with less editing needed. ChatGPT was faster for social repurposing and short-form content. I'd use Claude as my primary drafting tool and ChatGPT for quick repurposing tasks.
Did you disclose to readers that the posts were AI-assisted?
Yes — every post had a footer disclosure: "This post was researched and refined with AI tools." I believe transparency here is good practice, and there's no evidence it negatively affects reader trust when the content is genuinely useful.
Is there a risk that Google will penalise AI-generated content?
Google's stated position is that it evaluates content quality, not content origin. Thin, repetitive, or inaccurate AI content gets penalised — the same as any thin human-written content. The safeguard is the same: add real insight, verify facts, write for the reader.
What was the biggest mistake you made during the experiment?
Publishing two posts with less than my usual editing review because I was short on time. They were objectively fine posts — but they lacked the one real insight that makes content memorable. Don't skip the human-insight step, even when you're rushed.
Would this workflow work for a team, not just a solo creator?
Yes — and it scales better in a team context. The AI handles research and drafting, one team member handles the editorial review and adds original insight, another handles SEO and publishing. The 55-minute solo workflow becomes a 25-minute per-person workflow with two people.
